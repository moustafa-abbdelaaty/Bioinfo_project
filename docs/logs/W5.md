# Week 5 — Genome Assembly Principles (Theory)

## 1. Short-read vs Long-read Sequencing

### Illumina (Short-read)
- **Read length:** around 150-300 base pairs
- **Accuracy:** very high (Q30+ means 1 in 1000 error probability)
- **Main drawback:** reads are very short, so if the genome contains a repeat region longer than the read itself, the assembler cannot determine exactly where that region belongs in the genome
- like trying to build a puzzle from very small pieces where many pieces look identical.

### Oxford Nanopore (Long-read / ONT)
- **Read length:** from thousands to tens of thousands of bases, sometimes more
- **Accuracy:** lower than Illumina (especially in raw reads before correction)
- **Main advantage:** since the read itself is very long, it can "span" across an entire repeat region and reach the unique sequence on the other side 
- meaning it can resolve ambiguities that short-reads cannot.

### Why are both sometimes used together?
Many pipelines use Nanopore to build the overall genome structure (since it resolves repeats), then use Illumina to "correct" small sequence errors afterward 
— a process called **polishing** (like the Pilon tool we saw in the course materials).

## 2. Assembly Algorithms: Two Core Concepts

### de Bruijn Graphs
- The method typically used with **short-reads**
- The idea: each read is broken down into small overlapping fragments called **k-mers** (e.g., if k=21, each read becomes a series of 21-character fragments)
- The assembler then builds a "graph" connecting these k-mers based on their overlaps, and tries to walk through the graph from start to end to reconstruct the full sequence
- **The problem:** if there's a repeat longer than k, the graph gets confused and can't tell the difference between copies

### Overlap-Layout-Consensus (OLC)
- The method typically used with **long-reads** (like Nanopore)
- Three steps:
  1. **Overlap:** compare every read against every other read, looking for overlaps
  2. **Layout:** arrange the overlapping reads into one logical sequence based on those overlaps
  3. **Consensus:** where multiple reads cover the same region, pick the "consensus" (most common base) to reduce errors
- **The advantage:** since reads are long, fewer overlaps need to be analyzed and the signal is clearer

## 4.Review Questions

**1. Why is Nanopore better than Illumina at resolving repeats?**
Nanopore sequencing generates long reads (often tens to hundreds of kilobases long), whereas Illumina produces short reads (usually 150-300 bases). 
Repetitive regions in a genome are often much longer than short reads. 
Nanopore's long reads can span entirely across these repetitive regions and anchor themselves into the unique, non-repeating sequences on either side. 
This allows the assembly algorithms to know exactly where the repeat belongs and its true size. Short reads, on the other hand, fall entirely within the repeat, 
causing the assembly graph to tangle because the algorithm cannot determine how many times the repeat occurs or what surrounds it.

**2. What are the two main drawbacks of Nanopore reads compared to Illumina?**
+ Higher Error Rate: Traditionally, Nanopore has a lower single-read accuracy (a higher base-calling error rate, 
  particularly when reading stretches of identical bases known as homopolymers) compared to Illumina's highly accurate reads (>99.9%).
+ Lower Throughput/Higher Cost for Massive Data: While the technology is rapidly improving, Illumina generally yields massively higher total sequencing output at a lower cost per base.
  If you need exceptionally deep coverage of a complex population or very large genome, Illumina is often more cost-effective.

**3. What does "genome size estimation" mean, and why is it important before assembly?**
Genome size estimation is the process of calculating the approximate total number of DNA base pairs in an organism before you actually attempt to piece the genome together.
It is crucial for three main reasons:

+  It allows you to calculate the required sequencing coverage (Coverage = Total Sequenced Bases / Genome Size).

+  It helps you set the correct mathematical parameters and memory limits for your assembly software.

+  It provides a baseline benchmark to verify if your final assembled genome is complete. (For example, if your estimation says the genome is 2 Gigabases,
   but your final assembly is only 1 Gigabase, you know a significant portion of the genome is missing).

**4. What's the practical difference between de Bruijn graphs and OLC in terms of which data type each suits?**
+  de Bruijn graphs: This algorithmic approach is perfectly suited for short-read data (like Illumina). 
   It works by breaking millions of short reads into even smaller, fixed-length overlapping fragments called k-mers to build a continuous graph. 
   It is highly memory-efficient for managing massive amounts of short, highly accurate data.
+  Overlap-Layout-Consensus (OLC): As your notes outline, this approach is suited for long-read data (like Nanopore). 
   It directly compares the full length of every read against every other read to find overlaps. This requires too much computational power for millions of tiny short reads, 
   but it is perfect for long reads because it uses the clear, long overlapping signals to construct the layout and smooth out the higher error rates during the consensus step.
